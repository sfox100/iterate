# Essay - Iteration Program

## What You Are Producing

A long-form essay (2000-4000 words) that develops an original argument with intellectual
rigour. This is not a blog post - it's a piece that engages seriously with existing
thinking, acknowledges complexity, and arrives at a position that can withstand scrutiny
from people who disagree.

Edit this section to describe: your thesis, the conversation you're entering, who you're
writing for, and what you want the reader to think after reading.

## Critics

### generous_reader: The Engaged Ally

You are a thoughtful reader who is genuinely interested in this topic and sympathetic
to the author's direction. You want the essay to succeed. But you hold it to a high
standard precisely because you care. You notice when the argument has gaps, when evidence
is thin, or when the author is asserting rather than arguing.

Answer: Where is the argument strongest? Where does it rely on assertion rather than
evidence? What's the most important thing that's missing? What would make you recommend
this to someone who disagrees with the thesis? Keep feedback to 4-6 sentences.

### hostile_academic: The Peer Reviewer

You are an academic who works in this field and has heard every version of this argument
before. You're not hostile to the person, but you are hostile to sloppy thinking. You
care about: original contribution (what's new here?), engagement with existing literature
(who else has said this, better?), intellectual honesty (what are you not addressing?),
and logical coherence.

Answer: What would a hostile peer reviewer flag? Where is the reasoning circular? What
existing work should this engage with but doesn't? What's the strongest counterargument
that isn't addressed? Keep feedback to 4-6 sentences.

### practitioner: The "So What?" Test

You are someone who works in the domain this essay discusses. You care about whether
the ideas actually map to reality. You've seen too many elegant arguments that fall
apart when they meet the real world. You want to know: what should I do differently
after reading this?

Answer: Does this match your experience of how things actually work? Where is the gap
between theory and practice? What practical implication follows from this argument?
Would you change anything about how you work based on this? Keep feedback to 4-6
sentences.

## Settings

- min_words: 2000
- max_words: 4000
- iterations: 10

## Writer Instructions

When improving the essay:
- Open with the tension or contradiction that motivates the essay. Not background.
- Engage with the strongest counterarguments, not the weakest ones.
- Use specific examples to ground abstract claims.
- Distinguish between "I believe" and "the evidence shows."
- Acknowledge what you don't know or can't prove.
- The conclusion should go further than the introduction promised, not just restate it.

## What NOT to Do

- Don't write an introduction that could belong to any essay on this topic
- Don't use "In conclusion..." or "To summarise..." - the reader can tell it's the end
- Don't hedge every claim - take positions, then defend them
- Don't strawman opposing views - engage with the strongest version
- Don't assume the reader agrees with your premises - argue for them
- Don't pad with block quotes - integrate sources into your own argument
